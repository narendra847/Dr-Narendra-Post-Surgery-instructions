Hiatal Hernia Repair

Laparoscopic Hiatal Hernia Repair

What Was Done

A hiatal hernia happens when part of your stomach pushes up through the opening in your diaphragm (the muscle that separates your chest from your tummy). This can cause heartburn and reflux.

Your surgeon repaired this through keyhole surgery by pulling your stomach back down and closing the opening in your diaphragm. A fundoplication was also performed, where the top part of your stomach was wrapped around the bottom of your food pipe to create a valve that stops acid reflux.

Managing Your Pain

For the First Week

Some discomfort around your cuts is normal. Take these regularly:

  • Paracetamol - Follow the packet instructions (usually 2 tablets every 6 hours)
  • Ibuprofen - Take with food (usually 400mg every 8 hours)

Important: Large tablets or capsules must be crushed or dissolved in water for the first few weeks.

When to Use Stronger Pain Medicine

Only use your prescription pain medicine (opiates) if pain stops you from:

  • Walking
  • Getting out of bed
  • Taking deep breaths or coughing

Your Special Diet Plan

VERY IMPORTANT

Following this diet plan is crucial for your recovery!

Your stomach and food pipe need time to heal. You must follow this 6-week diet plan carefully to avoid complications.

1-2

Weeks 1-2: Free Fluids Only

Can you drink it through a straw? Then you can have it!

You CAN have:

  • • Water, tea, coffee
  • • Clear soups and broths
  • • Fruit juices (no pulp)
  • • Milk, milkshakes, smoothies
  • • Protein shakes
  • • Jelly, ice cream
  • • Sports drinks

You CANNOT have:

  • • Any solid food
  • • Thick smoothies with chunks
  • • Fruit with pulp or seeds
  • • Anything you need to chew
3-4

Weeks 3-4: Pureed Foods

Baby food consistency - completely smooth, no lumps

You CAN have:

  • • Blended soups
  • • Mashed potato (very smooth)
  • • Pureed vegetables
  • • Pureed meat or fish
  • • Yogurt, custard
  • • Porridge (well-cooked)
  • • Baby food

You CANNOT have:

  • • Any lumps or chunks
  • • Rice or pasta
  • • Bread
  • • Raw vegetables or fruits
  • • Nuts or seeds
5-6

Weeks 5-6: Soft Foods

Can you mush it with a spoon? Then you can eat it!

You CAN have:

  • • Scrambled eggs
  • • Soft fish (no bones)
  • • Minced meat in gravy
  • • Well-cooked pasta
  • • Soft vegetables
  • • Ripe bananas

You CANNOT have:

  • • Tough meats
  • • Raw vegetables
  • • Nuts or seeds
  • • Hard bread or toast
  • • Anything crunchy or chewy

Important Eating Tips

  • Eat slowly and take small bites
  • Drink plenty of fluids between meals
  • Stop eating if you feel full or uncomfortable
  • Sit upright while eating and for 30 minutes after

Looking After Your Wounds

You CAN

  • Shower normally - Your wounds are sealed with medical glue
  • Wear normal clothes - No special dressing needed
  • Pat dry gently after showering

You CANNOT (for 4-6 weeks)

  • Have a bath - No soaking in water
  • Go swimming - Keep wounds dry
  • Use hot tubs or spas

When to See Your Doctor

Contact your local doctor if you notice:

Increasing Redness

Around wounds

Swelling

Puffiness around cuts

Increasing Pain

Pain getting worse

Special Warning: Nausea & Vomiting

It's very important that you do not vomit or retch after this surgery. If you feel sick, take your anti-nausea medicine immediately. Contact your doctor if nausea doesn't improve.

Getting Back to Normal

Moving Around

Start walking as soon as possible. Gentle movement helps recovery and prevents blood clots.

Follow-Up

You'll have a swallow test the morning after surgery. You'll need an appointment in 6 weeks to check your progress. Please call my private clinic to book this appointment.

When Can I Drive Again?

Follow these rules before getting back behind the wheel:

1. Wait at least 1 week

No driving for the first week after surgery

2. Test yourself first

Make sure you can comfortably wear a seatbelt and make an emergency stop without pain

3. No driving on opiates

Do not drive if you are taking prescription pain medicine (opiates)

4. Check with your insurance

Contact your insurance company to confirm you're covered to drive after surgery
